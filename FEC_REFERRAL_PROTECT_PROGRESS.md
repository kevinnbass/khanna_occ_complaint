## 1. Legal framework

This Complaint is brought against PROTECT PROGRESS (C00848440), a non-connected independent-expenditure-only political committee [FECPP_M001], on the question whether its 2024 communications in support of Representative Ro Khanna constituted coordinated communications under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21 and were therefore in-kind contributions exceeding the candidate-committee limits at 52 U.S.C. § 30116 [FECPP_M004, FECPP_M034]. The respondent is the spender; the candidate-side recipient (C00503185) is identified for context and discovery scope but the operative liability theory under FECA's coordination doctrine attaches first to PROTECT PROGRESS as the entity that paid for, approved, and disseminated the communications [FECPP_M001, FECPP_M002].

Under 52 U.S.C. § 30116(a)(7)(B)(i) and the implementing regulation at 11 C.F.R. § 109.21, an expenditure "made in cooperation, consultation, or concert with, or at the request or suggestion of" a candidate or the candidate's authorized political committee is treated as an in-kind contribution subject to the candidate-committee contribution limits, not as an independent expenditure [FECPP_M004]. The coordinated-versus-independent distinction was established in *Buckley v. Valeo*, 424 U.S. 1, 46–47 (1976) [FECPP_M045]. *Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310, 365 (2010), held that the BCRA § 203 prohibition on corporate-funded independent expenditures was unconstitutional [FECPP_M046]. The coordination framework promulgated at 11 C.F.R. § 109.21 has remained in force and continues to be the operative test the Commission applies to determine when a third-party expenditure is a coordinated communication and therefore an in-kind contribution.

The three-part test at 11 C.F.R. §109.21 asks whether:

1. **Payment** — a third party paid for the communication;
2. **Content** — the communication satisfies one of the content standards (e.g., a public communication referring to a clearly identified federal candidate within the 90- or 120-day windows); and
3. **Conduct** — the communication satisfies one of five conduct standards: (i) request or suggestion, (ii) material involvement, (iii) substantial discussion, (iv) common vendor, or (v) former employee. [FECPP_M004]

A Super PAC's independence turns on the absence of the conduct element. Where the same principals control or materially direct both the candidate committee and the Super PAC, or where donor relationships are sufficiently dense and Super-PAC activity tracks candidate priorities, that independence is a question the Commission can test on the public record and its subpoena authority.

Nothing in this Complaint contests the constitutionally protected right of corporations and other associations to make independent expenditures recognized in *Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310 (2010) [FECPP_M046], or the holding of *SpeechNow.org v. FEC*, 599 F.3d 686, 694–96 (D.C. Cir. 2010) (en banc), that the government cannot apply contribution limits to a political committee that makes only independent expenditures [FECPP_M047]; the Commission's task under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21 is to determine, on the public-record structural pattern alleged, whether the conduct test for independence is satisfied.

The conduct-prong analysis advanced here proceeds along two independent tracks. **On the Khanna side**, the operative conduct theory proceeds under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(d)(2)–(3) (material involvement / substantial discussion), anchored on the upstream FAIRSHAKE PAC – PROTECT PROGRESS shared-treasurer and shared-address structural facts and the eight § 18G affiliate transfers totaling $35.7M set out at § 2.1.1 [FECPP_M006, FECPP_M007]. The (d)(4) common-vendor pathway is not advanced on the Khanna side; full vendor-retention discovery is among the relief requested at § 5 [FECPP_M029]. **On the Gottheimer side**, the (d)(4) common-vendor presumption is independently established by the Global Strategy Group LLC dual-retention record set out at § 3.3, with two payment pairs falling within the regulation's 120-day window [FECPP_M028]. The two tracks are independently sufficient and may be addressed by the Commission separately or together.

### 2.8 Concurrent LDA-registered institutional-pressure channels operating in parallel on the SAB 121 axis — bounded foreign-principal disclosure under 22 U.S.C. § 613(h)

The contribution-side three-window envelope set out at § 2.7 above is one of two concurrent pressure surfaces operating on the SAB 121 axis during the same May 8, 2024 to July 11, 2024 vote-flip window. The second is the federal Lobbying Disclosure Act (LDA) institutional-contact surface. This sub-section sets that surface out for the Commission's review, identifies three concurrent channels that operated in parallel during the flip window, and bounds the legal framing so that the foreign-domicile of two of those channels is correctly characterized under the LDA exemption to the Foreign Agents Registration Act at 22 U.S.C. § 613(h) rather than as an unmet FARA registration obligation.

**The Q2 + Q3 2024 LDA contact universe on SAB 121.** Across LD-2 quarterly registrations filed for the second and third quarters of 2024 (filing_year='2024', filing_period IN ('second_quarter','third_quarter'), filing_type LIKE 'Q%') whose lobbying_activities body discloses contact on Staff Accounting Bulletin 121, on the digital-asset-custody policy axis, or on H.J.Res. 109 specifically, approximately thirty-five distinct LD-2 quarterly filings are recorded — twenty in the second quarter, fifteen in the third on the live `lake.lda_filings` substrate, with absolute counts subject to ± one-to-two filing fluctuation across LDA quarterly amendment cycles [FECPP_M022]. The aggregate disclosed lobby spend across those filings is approximately $17,379,000 ($9,069,000 in the second quarter; $8,310,000 in the third), summing reported income for outside-firm engagements with reported expenses for in-house lobbying [FECPP_M022]. Government-entity contacts disclosed on the SAB 121 activity rows are concentrated at the legislative-and-financial-regulator surface and include the House of Representatives, the Senate, the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Treasury, the Federal Reserve, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, the Internal Revenue Service, and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation; absolute per-agency activity-row counts at the live `lake.lda_activity_gov_entities` substrate fluctuate across LDA amendment cycles, with the load-bearing finding being the nine-agency coverage frame across the legislative chambers and the federal financial regulators administering the SAB 121 / digital-asset-custody program [FECPP_M022]. The crypto-advocacy entities documented at §§ 2.5 and 2.6 above — Blockchain Association, The Digital Chamber, DeFi Education Fund, Chamber of Progress — are present in this universe as registrants or clients [FECPP_M015, FECPP_M022].

**Three concurrent channels operating in parallel.** Three institutional-pressure channels operate within this LDA-disclosure universe in addition to the U.S.-domiciled crypto-advocacy quintet:

1. **Anchor Labs, Inc., and Anchorage Digital Bank** — Anchor Labs is the parent company of Coinbase Custody and is the operator of Anchorage Digital Bank, the federally-chartered crypto custody trust company [FECPP_M022]. Anchor Labs registered as an in-house LDA filer on the SAB 121 axis during this window and additionally engaged outside firms GrayRobinson PA and ACG Analytics for SAB 121–axis contact [FECPP_M022]. The covered-position pedigree disclosed on the Anchor Labs lobbying contacts includes Kevin Wysocki, whose disclosed prior-service record on the LD-2 covered-position field comprises Professional Staff, House Financial Services Committee (Rep. Andy Barr), Legislative Assistant to Representative Tom Emmer, and Legislative Assistant to Representative Michele Bachmann — all three offices Members or committee-and-staff offices with direct jurisdiction over SAB 121 / SEC accounting / digital-asset custody policy [FECPP_M022].

2. **European Banking Federation (EBF)** — a Brussels-domiciled trade association of European national banking associations [FECPP_M022, FECPP_M023]. EBF filed LD-2 disclosures during this window reporting SAB 121–axis contact with the House of Representatives, the Senate, the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Federal Reserve, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency [FECPP_M022]. EBF's LD-2 form discloses the foreign-affiliation interest in the lobbying activity directly on the registrant and client identification fields — the LDA's principal-identification prong — recording country code "BE" / Belgium on both the registrant and client country and principal-place-of-business country attributes filed with the Secretary of the Senate and the Clerk of the House [FECPP_M023].

3. **Tether Operations / Tether Holdings S.A. de C.V.** — the El Salvador–domiciled stablecoin issuer whose U.S. lobbying-vehicle cohort engaged six retained outside firms across approximately twenty-seven LD-2 filings 2024 through the first quarter of 2026, naming ten covered-position lobbyists in lobbying_activities [FECPP_M023]. The Tether channel is distinguished from the other two by its outside-firm-only contact architecture (no in-house Tether registrant on the U.S. lobbying surface) and by the foreign-domicile that the retained-firm LD-2 disclosures attribute to the principal in the client country attributes — country code "SV" / El Salvador for Tether Operations / Tether Holdings S.A. de C.V., and country code "VG" / British Virgin Islands for Tether Operations Limited [FECPP_M023]. Tether is not itself a contributor to PROTECT PROGRESS, to FAIRSHAKE PAC, or to Ro For Congress (C00503185); the Tether channel is, on the Commission-record state of the filings, an LDA-registered institutional-pressure channel operating on the same SAB 121 / digital-asset custody policy axis on which the candidate-recipient flipped his vote [FECPP_M020, FECPP_M023].

**Bounding the foreign-principal framing under 22 U.S.C. § 613(h).** The foreign-domicile of two of the three channels above (EBF, Brussels; Tether Holdings, San Salvador) presents the question whether the Foreign Agents Registration Act at 22 U.S.C. §§ 611 et seq. attaches independently of the LDA disclosure regime. The Commission's coordination inquiry need not, and as a matter of statutory architecture should not, treat these channels as carrying an unmet FARA registration obligation. 22 U.S.C. § 613(h) — the LDA exemption from FARA — provides that "any agent of a person described in section 611(b)(2) of this title or an entity described in section 611(b)(3) of this title if the agent has engaged in lobbying activities and has registered under the Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 in connection with the agent's representation of such person or entity" is exempt from FARA's § 612(a) registration requirement. Tether Holdings S.A. de C.V. (a private corporation organized under the laws of El Salvador) and the European Banking Federation (a private trade association organized under the laws of Belgium) each fit the § 611(b)(3) "entity organized under the laws of a foreign country" definition [FECPP_M023]. Their retained outside firms have, on the face of the LD-2 filings, registered under the LDA in connection with their representation of those entities [FECPP_M023]. The § 613(h) exemption facially applies; FARA registration is not the operative statutory hook [FECPP_M023, FECPP_M042].

The companion criminal statute at 18 U.S.C. § 951 reaches "agent of a foreign GOVERNMENT" specifically — its subsection (d) requires "agreement to operate within the United States subject to the direction or control of a foreign government or official." Neither EBF nor Tether is a foreign government or an official thereof; absent specific evidence of direction or control by the Government of Belgium, the European Union, or the Government of El Salvador (which the LD-2 filings, on their face, do not establish), § 951 does not attach to either channel [FECPP_M043]. The foreign-national-contribution prohibition at 52 U.S.C. § 30121 is also not implicated [FECPP_M035]: the institutional-contact channel disclosed under LDA does not, by itself, generate any foreign-national contribution, and the candidate-side individual-contribution ledger reviewed at § 2.7 above contains no contribution of foreign-national origin.

**The Commission-relevant inference.** The significance of this sub-section for the coordination inquiry is the existence of a three-channel LDA-registered institutional-pressure surface — Anchor Labs / EBF / Tether — operating in parallel with the U.S.-domiciled crypto-advocacy quintet documented at §§ 2.5 and 2.6, on the same SAB 121 vote-flip axis described at § 2.7, during the same Q2 + Q3 2024 window [FECPP_M020, FECPP_M022, FECPP_M023]. The candidate-side conduct-prong inquiry under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(d)(1)–(d)(3) confronts a contact-and-pressure environment of substantially greater density on the SAB 121 axis than the three-window contribution envelope alone displays, and the foreign-affiliation flag on two of those three channels is correctly disclosed under 22 U.S.C. § 613(h)'s LDA safe harbor, not concealed. The Complainant does not allege that any LDA-registered lobbying contact disclosed in this universe is itself unlawful, nor that registration under the § 613(h) safe harbor is itself coordination evidence within the meaning of 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(d). The findings present to the Commission the parallel institutional-contact density relevant to the conduct-prong analysis at § 2.7 above, and make clear on the face of this Complaint that the foreign-domicile of two of the three concurrent channels is statutorily addressed by an existing safe harbor rather than by an unmet registration obligation that the Complainant has chosen not to plead.

### 5.1 Revealed-preference cross-candidate substitution as a Commission-applicable framework — relevance to the 2026-cycle deployment record

The relief sought above rests on the 2024-cycle factual record set out at §§ 2 and 3. PROTECT PROGRESS's subsequent 2026-cycle deployment record addresses the same coordination questions through a framework the Commission has applied in prior independent-expenditure analyses, and the Complainant respectfully requests that the Commission apply that framework here [FECPP_M027]. This sub-section names the framework, bounds it, and identifies the 2026-cycle record on which it operates.

**The framework.** Where a non-connected independent-expenditure-only political committee deploys substantial expenditures in a competitive primary contest in which (i) one or more candidates have publicly solicited or accepted contributions from the committee's funding industry, (ii) the targeted opponent has not solicited or accepted such contributions, and (iii) the targeted opponent has no documented public-record statement or vote opposing the committee's funding industry's policy interests, the deployment evidences targeting by **revealed-preference cross-candidate substitution** rather than by candidate-statement response [FECPP_M027]. The candidate-defeat purpose is supplied by the partisan-faction posture of the targeted opponent relative to the supported opponents in the same field [FECPP_M027]. The distinction matters for the coordination inquiry because partisan-faction-signaling deployment is further from First-Amendment-protected issue advocacy and policy-information dissemination — the protected category recognized in *Buckley v. Valeo*, 424 U.S. 1, 47 (1976) [FECPP_M045], *FEC v. Massachusetts Citizens for Life, Inc.*, 479 U.S. 238 (1986) [FECPP_M049], and *Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310, 359–60 (2010) — and closer to candidate-defeat-purpose deployment, which the Commission's coordination regulations at 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(c) and (d) are designed to capture as in-kind contributions where conduct standards are satisfied [FECPP_M004].
