## 2. 18 U.S.C. § 207 — Post-employment restrictions

### 2.1 Statutory framework

4. 18 U.S.C. § 207(a)(1) prohibits a former senior executive-branch employee from knowingly making any communication to, or appearance before, his former agency on behalf of any other person in connection with a particular matter in which the United States is a party or has a direct and substantial interest and in which the former employee participated personally and substantially. § 207(c)(1) restricts senior officials for one year post-service. § 207(e) extends certain restrictions to senior legislative-branch officials.

## 3. 18 U.S.C. § 1001 — Lobbying-Disclosure-Act § 203 semiannual filings

### 3.1 Statutory framework

8. 18 U.S.C. § 1001 prohibits knowingly making a materially false statement in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch. LDA § 203 semiannual contribution reports (Form LD-203) are filed under penalty of perjury pursuant to 2 U.S.C. § 1604(d) and are federal filings within § 1001's reach.

### 3.2 Factual basis

9. A population of LD-203 filings spanning 2011 through 2025 identify respondent's principal committee or an affiliated committee as the recipient of aggregated contributions from registered lobbyists, totaling approximately **$299,198** across 147 line items filed by 53 unique LDA-registered organizations. Five lobbying firms are concurrently registered as Foreign Agents Registration Act ("FARA") agents and as Lobbying Disclosure Act domestic lobbyists, and each files LD-203 reports identifying respondent's committee as a recipient during a period of simultaneous foreign-principal activity.

10. The § 1001 review question is whether the filings' contribution-amount aggregations, lobbying-activity descriptions, and registrant-status attestations are accurate and non-misleading — particularly for the five dual FARA / LDA registrants where foreign-principal activity is co-located with the domestic contribution disclosure. This office has not conducted an internal-accuracy audit of the LD-203 filings; the referral is submitted for the Department's review of whether any material discrepancy exists between the disclosed figures and the underlying financial records accessible through Department subpoena authority.

## 4. 18 U.S.C. § 1346 — Honest services (contingent)

### 4.1 Statutory framework

11. 18 U.S.C. § 1346 defines a "scheme or artifice to defraud" for purposes of 18 U.S.C. §§ 1341, 1343, and 1344 to include a scheme or artifice to deprive another of the intangible right of honest services. Applied to a federal officeholder, § 1346 reaches concealment of a financial conflict of interest where the concealment deprives the public of the officeholder's honest services. 18 U.S.C. § 201 (bribery of public officials) is referenced here only as the companion statutory framework should the Department wish to examine any quid-pro-quo construction; no § 201 conduct is alleged on the public record.

### 4.2 Contingent factual basis

12. The referral under § 1346 is contingent on the House Committee on Ethics making a 5 U.S.C. § 13104(d)(1)(A) scope finding (drawing on Office of Government Ethics Letter Opinion 03-2 and Department of Justice Advisory Opinion 170 as persuasive secondary authority) that the Ahuja Charitable Foundation's approximately $45 million corpus constitutes a spouse-held or spouse-controlled asset required to be disclosed on respondent's Annual Personal Financial Disclosure. The parallel material filed contemporaneously with the Office of Congressional Conduct (`HOUSE_ETHICS_SUBMISSION_KHANNA.md`) presents that scope question for the Committee on Ethics's consideration upon Office-of-Congressional-Conduct referral.

13. The § 1346 substrate, should the Committee on Ethics scope finding issue, rests on three affirmative findings and one exculpatory finding, which the Department is asked to consider together:

   (a) **Spouse-trustee presence.** Ritu Ahuja Khanna is named as a Trustee of the Ahuja Charitable Foundation (EIN 34-1685088) in Internal Revenue Service Form 990-PF, Part VIII (Officer / Trustee schedule), for tax years 2018 through 2024 — seven consecutive tax years, the full window of the Foundation's e-file 990-PF filings in the IRS Tax Exempt Organization e-file corpus, with title, hours per week (5), and compensation ($0) constant across all seven years (each entry verifiable against `lake.irs_990_officers`). Part VIII requires listing of all officers and trustees without a compensation threshold, so the Part VIII entry is dispositive on the spouse's formal role.

   (b) **Dover, Delaware rental — ten-year asymmetric disclosure.** Respondent's TY2021 Annual PFD Schedule D discloses a $100,001–$250,000 mortgage liability on a Dover, Delaware rental property. The matching Schedule A asset line and Schedule B or Schedule C rental-income line are **absent from all ten Annual PFDs covering TY2014 through TY2023**. Across that same ten-PFD corpus, six of seven other rental properties held by respondent are correctly paired with asset and income lines — rebutting any "reasonable interpretation" or "benign oversight" defense to the Dover asymmetry. The Dover asymmetry is a clean 5 U.S.C. § 13104(a)(1)(B) and § 13104(a)(3) defect independent of the Foundation scope question and demonstrates a concrete disclosure-omission pattern bearing on the honest-services concealment inquiry.

   (c) **Passive-structure defense foreclosed.** Respondent's Annual PFDs for TY2017 through TY2020 disclose a continuous Goldman Sachs margin facility on the spouse-owned brokerage account: TY2017 "over $1 million"; TY2018 two Goldman margin entries combining $265,000 to $550,000; TY2019 a $100,001–$250,000 margin entry from the Ritu Ahuja 1994 Trust (a spouse-affiliated family trust) alongside a Goldman margin entry of $250,001 to $500,000; TY2020 combined margin exposure of $500,001 to $1 million across two liabilities. The same accounts execute **282 separate XSP (S&P 500 mini-index) PUT-option transactions** across respondent's tenure, each band-disclosed $1,001–$15,000, with strikes systematically out-of-the-money and expiries rolling continuously — the footprint of a systematic cash-secured or margined short-volatility premium-harvest program. Short-PUT writing on a cash-secured or margined account requires Regulation T and FINRA Rule 4210 options-level authorization executed by the account holder (the Member's spouse), not by a discretionary trustee. That authorization, combined with the Goldman margin exposure, is structurally incompatible with a qualified-blind-trust, exempt-investment-fund, or separately-managed-account posture under 5 U.S.C. § 13104(f)(3).

   (d) **Exculpatory — respondent is not named as a foundation trustee.** Respondent is **not** named in Part VIII of any lake-covered Ahuja Charitable Foundation Form 990-PF for TY2018, TY2019, TY2020, TY2021, or TY2024. Because Part VIII requires listing of all officers and trustees without a compensation threshold, the negative search is dispositive on any premise that respondent himself holds a foundation-trustee role. The § 1346 predicate therefore rests solely on the spouse-held and spouse-controlled corpus analysis, not on any respondent-held trustee role.

14. If the House Committee on Ethics issues the scope finding at § 13104(d)(1)(A), and if respondent's Annual PFDs 2017–2024 did not disclose the Foundation corpus or the spouse-trustee interest in the manner the Committee determines is required, the § 1346 question presented is whether the non-disclosure — read together with the ten-year Dover asymmetry and the margin-and-options scaffold — reflects knowing concealment. Developing that inquiry requires (i) the Committee scope determination; (ii) Foundation internal-governance records establishing the scope of the spouse's actual authority over the corpus; (iii) subpoena of respondent's PFD-preparation communications across the relevant years; and (iv) subpoena of joint household tax returns for TY2014 through TY2024 to reconcile the Dover Delaware rental treatment against Schedule E (supplemental income).

15. This office does not allege § 1346 conduct on the public record. The referral preserves the question for the Department in the event the House Committee on Ethics scope finding issues.

## 5. 15 U.S.C. § 78u-1(g) — STOCK Act §§ 3–4 material non-public information

### 5.1 Statutory framework

16. 15 U.S.C. § 78u-1(g), added by the Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act of 2012 §§ 3–4, P.L. 112-105, extends Rule 10b-5 and Exchange Act § 10(b) prohibitions to Members of Congress who trade on the basis of material non-public information acquired in connection with legislative or oversight duties. 15 U.S.C. § 78ff supplies the corresponding criminal penalty provision. *United States v. Salman*, 580 U.S. 39 (2016); *Dirks v. SEC*, 463 U.S. 646 (1983); and *United States v. Chiarella*, 445 U.S. 222 (1980) supply the operative materiality, breach-of-fiduciary-duty, and tipper-tippee doctrines.

### 5.2 Factual basis — three-regulatory-axis convergence

17. The companion complaint Counts 1 and 3 develop the following absolute-count convergence, in each case tested against the full House chamber:

   - **Same-day SEC Form 8-K intersection.** 186 distinct household trades executed on the same calendar day that the issuer filed a Form 8-K; chamber rank one of ninety-six Members with twenty or more ticker-bridged transactions. The rate dimension is disclosed candidly across both reference universes: 5.42 percent, chamber rank thirty-six of ninety-six (P62, above chamber median 4.50 percent and below chamber P75 6.93 percent) and active-trader cohort P89 (above cohort P75 4.77 percent and below the cohort top decile). The probative force rests on the conjunction of the absolute-count finding with the household's concentration on issuers in sectors under House Armed Services, House Oversight, and House Science-Space-and-Technology committee jurisdiction, and on the composite-pattern parallelism with the NDAA-enactment, FDA Advisory Committee, CMS-rulemaking, and aligned-officer Form 3/4/5 same-day intersections, not on rate-dimension outlier-uniqueness. *See* Exhibit M.

   - **Aligned-direction named-officer same-day intersection.** 86 household trades executed on the same calendar day that a named corporate officer at the same issuer filed a same-direction Form 3, 4, or 5; chamber rank three of one hundred fifty-six, with only two Members ranked higher by count. The rate dimension: 1.20 percent, chamber P70. *See* Exhibit Z.

   - **Pharmaceutical × FDA Advisory Committee.** 4,595 household pharmaceutical-sector trades within ±14 days of an FDA Advisory Committee meeting and 1,070 within ±3 days; chamber **rank one of sixty-six** Members on both window widths, with a 6.1× lead over the second-ranked Member on the ±14-day test and a 6.4× lead on the ±3-day test. Ninety-seven percent of the household's 839 pharmaceutical-sector transactions fall within ±14 days of some FDA Advisory Committee event. *See* Exhibit X.

   - **Pharmaceutical × CMS rulemaking.** 1,244 household pharmaceutical-sector transactions within ±14 days of a discrete CMS rulemaking event; chamber **rank one of sixty-six**, 14× chamber P95 (89). *See* Exhibit F.

The convergence across three independent regulatory bodies — the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Food and Drug Administration, and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services — is particularly probative. Respondent serves on the Committee on Oversight and Government Reform (which oversees the relevant agencies) and sits on the Committee on Armed Services (which authorizes the Defense Department contract vehicles at issue in Count 2). The same committee portfolios that confer oversight on these regulators are the committee portfolios on which respondent serves.

### 5.3 Factual basis — NDAA enactment-window defense-prime cluster

18. Count 2 of the companion complaint documents fourteen household defense-prime trades clustered on four distinct NDAA enactment dates between November 2017 and December 2023, accompanied by a household NAY vote on twelve of thirteen NDAA passage votes across 2017 through 2024. Respondent's household accumulated defense-prime common stock on enactment-window dates while respondent voted publicly against the enabling legislation. *See* Exhibit E.

19. Count 3 further documents at least nine calendar days on which Palantir Technologies received a federal contract action and respondent's household executed a PLTR trade, including the May 10, 2022 U.S. Air Force $18.9 million contract announcement paired with six intra-day dependent-child PLTR trades and the September 19, 2025 three-agency cluster (Department of Homeland Security Immigration and Customs Enforcement $19.4 million; Internal Revenue Service $4.5 million; Department of Health and Human Services $0.4 million) paired with a dependent-child PLTR BUY. Palantir's lifetime federal-contract exposure over respondent's tenure is approximately $4.88 billion. *See* Exhibit N.

### 5.4 Factual basis — the August 2, 2024 CMS simultaneous-trade cluster

20. On August 2, 2024 — thirteen days before CMS's August 15, 2024 publication of the Final Part D Negotiated Drugs List for 2026 — respondent's household executed a 286-transaction broad rebalance that includes simultaneous transactions in four of the nine unique pharmaceutical manufacturers whose drug appeared on the published list (AbbVie, Amgen, Johnson & Johnson, Merck) alongside seven additional pharmaceutical-sector positions (Moderna, Regeneron, Vertex, Incyte, Gilead, Biogen, and an aggregated additional pharma-prime line item) — eleven pharmaceutical positions in total within the larger rebalance. The list contents on August 2, 2024 were still pre-publication and remained within the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services' and Department of Health and Human Services' confidential advance review. The four-of-nine same-day overlap is, on its own, in the range of the hypergeometric expected hit-rate from a 248-distinct-asset rebalance against a nine-manufacturer industry sub-list; the request to develop this paragraph rests on the conjunction of that overlap with the thirteen-day pre-publication window, the seven-year repeating-pattern characteristic across fourteen rulemaking episodes, the Oversight Committee jurisdictional nexus, and the independent three-way tracker corroboration of eleven of twelve cited 2024-08-02 trades. *See* Exhibit F § 3 (full table and candor paragraph); Exhibit FF (per-trade three-way validation).

### 5.5 Review question for the Department

21. The review question under 15 U.S.C. § 78u-1(g) is whether the three-regulatory-axis absolute-count convergence, the NDAA cluster, the Palantir same-day contract-action cluster, and the August 2, 2024 simultaneous manufacturer cluster reflect trading on material non-public information acquired in connection with respondent's legislative or oversight duties. The review is confined to common-stock and committee-window patterns; no deep-out-of-the-money options pattern is presented.

22. Developing the § 78u-1(g) and § 78ff inquiry requires subpoena authority over (i) brokerage trade-timing metadata, (ii) account-control and communications records at the spouse-owned and dependent-child accounts, (iii) Palantir federal-contract pre-award communications, and (iv) respondent's congressional communications in the period immediately preceding the clustered trade dates.
