# EXHIBIT Q — AHUJA FOUNDATION GRANTEES AND THEIR FEDERAL LOBBYING FOOTPRINT

**Case**: *In re Representative Rohit "Ro" Khanna (CA-17)*
**Count supported**: Count 6 (Ethics in Government Act 5 U.S.C. § 13104(d)(1)(A); House Rule XXVI); corroborates Count 3 (HHS / CMS jurisdictional nexus)

---

## 1. Methodology

For each top grantee of the Ahuja Charitable Foundation (EIN 34-1685088) during tax years 2020 through 2024 — the window covered by the IRS Form 990-PF e-file corpus for this filer — the count of federal lobbying filings is drawn from the Senate Lobbying Disclosure Act filings database (`lake.lda_filings`), keyed on the grantee's name in the LDA `client` field. For the two largest healthcare grantees, the top issue codes across lifetime LDA filings are reported. Issue codes are then cross-referenced against respondent's House committee jurisdictions (House Armed Services; House Oversight and Government Reform; House Budget).

The hypothesis tested: whether a non-trivial share of the Ahuja Foundation's grant flow routes to institutions that concurrently lobby Congress on issues within respondent's committee jurisdictions. A negative result would itself be a publishable finding — a family foundation whose grantees do not lobby the Member.

## 4. Overlap with respondent's committee jurisdictions

Respondent has served on the House Armed Services Committee, the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform / Oversight and Accountability, and the House Budget Committee during his five terms in the 115th through 119th Congresses. The Ahuja Foundation grantee issue codes that intersect those jurisdictions:

| Lobbying issue code | Respondent's committee with jurisdictional overlap | University Hospitals filings | Cleveland Clinic filings |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Health Issues | Oversight cross-cutting authority over HHS, FDA, NIH | 72 | 123 |
| Medicare / Medicaid | Oversight cross-cutting authority over CMS | 37 | 99 |
| Budget / Appropriations | Budget Committee, on which respondent serves directly | 46 | 51 |
| Medical / Disease Research / Clinical Labs | Oversight authority over NIH | 36 | 10 |

Both top healthcare grantees file LDA disclosures on Medicare / Medicaid and Budget / Appropriations — issue areas falling within the Oversight and Budget Committee jurisdictions respondent holds. The per-filing question of whether University Hospitals, Cleveland Clinic, or any other grantee has ever directly lobbied respondent's office specifically is a separate inquiry that requires per-filing review of the `lobbying_contacts` field in each LDA quarterly report. That per-filing review is deferred; the structural finding does not depend on it.

## 5. The structural interest

The chain connects as follows:

1. Respondent's spouse, **Ritu Ahuja Khanna**, is a named trustee of the Ahuja Charitable Foundation and a Schedule B substantial contributor to it, as documented on the Foundation's Form 990-PF officer schedules and Schedule B for tax years 2018 through 2024. See **Exhibit H**.
2. The Foundation has paid, in deduplicated totals for tax years 2020 through 2024, **$3,906,000 to University Hospitals (Cleveland) across 10 grants and $1,120,000 to NCH (Naples)**, together with smaller grants to Cleveland Clinic ($200,000), Lake County Free Clinic ($125,000), Johns Hopkins ($50,000), Georgetown ($75,000), University of Pennsylvania ($60,000), and Columbia ($30,000) — a philanthropic footprint concentrated in Cleveland, Naples, and Washington, D.C. The five-tax-year aggregate of all Foundation grants in this window is **$7,423,252 across 185 grants**.
3. **Six of those eleven grantees file federal lobbying disclosures at sustained quarterly cadence**, predominantly on Health Issues, Medicare / Medicaid, and Budget / Appropriations — issues within respondent's Oversight and Budget Committee jurisdictions.
4. The respondent's household therefore has a second-degree institutional interest in legislative outcomes the grantee organizations lobby for: CMS reimbursement policy that benefits academic medical centers can affect the operating posture of the institutions that the Foundation's multi-million-dollar grant flow supports.

This exhibit does **not** allege a quid pro quo. It documents a structural conflict-of-interest surface that is relevant to the § 13104(d)(1)(A) spouse-asset disclosure question carried by Count 6 and to the jurisdictional-nexus element of Count 3 (household trading in sectors subject to respondent's committees of jurisdiction).

## 6. What this exhibit does not claim

The following evidence is not developed in this exhibit and is not required for its structural finding:

- Direct lobbying contact between University Hospitals or Cleveland Clinic and respondent's office specifically — requires per-filing `lobbying_contacts` field extraction.
- Specific legislation within respondent's committees that University Hospitals or Cleveland Clinic lobbied on during the years the Foundation was actively granting.
- Any vote by respondent on healthcare appropriations legislation that materially affected University Hospitals or Cleveland Clinic federal reimbursement or grant streams during the same years.

Each of those three lines of evidence, if developed in a subsequent review, would promote the present structural finding into a documented quid-pro-quo predicate. The exhibit records the structural finding now; it queues the per-filing review for the appropriate committee or agency.

This exhibit makes no claim under 26 U.S.C. § 4945 standing alone. The Internal Revenue Code's taxable-expenditure framework for private foundations turns on whether the foundation's own funds were expended for non-charitable lobbying or attempts to influence legislation, not on whether the foundation's grantees independently lobby Congress. The structural finding above is a record of grantee-side lobbying activity for purposes of the Ethics in Government Act § 13104(d)(1)(A) spouse-asset-and-position disclosure question (Count 6) and the jurisdictional-nexus element of Count 3; it is not a § 4945 attribution claim by the complainant. The narrower question whether any specific Foundation expenditure could implicate § 4941 (self-dealing) or § 4945(d)(1) (grants to organizations that themselves lobby, where the foundation has not satisfied the expenditure-responsibility safe harbor) is preserved as the question for Internal Revenue Service Tax Exempt Organization investigation, not pleaded here as a finding.
