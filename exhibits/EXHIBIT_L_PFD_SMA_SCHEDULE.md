## 1. Purpose

Respondent has publicly attributed household trading activity to a separately managed account ("SMA") structure in which a trustee or broker exercises discretion without consulting the Member. This exhibit establishes, from the face of respondent's own sworn 2024 Annual PFD, that **no such SMA is disclosed on the filing**. The SMA affirmative defense is therefore factually unavailable, and the complaint's Counts 1, 2, 3, and 7 proceed on a rebutted footing.

## 3.1 Cross-confirmation in the household Periodic Transaction Report record

The PFD-side observation in §3 — that no separately managed account, qualified blind trust, or third-party discretionary custodian appears on the 2024 Annual PFD — does not stand on the PFD alone. The PTR-side disclosure record therefore contains the same family-trust grammar that appears on the 2024 Annual PFD asset catalog — extended across a decade of household trading. The PFD-and-PTR record therefore agrees: every disclosed common-stock trade in the household originates from a family-trust wrapper. The SMA-defense premise — that a third-party broker is making the trade decisions — has no documentary footing in either filing stream.

## 4. Why this matters

The portfolio on the PFD consists of private-equity, hedge-fund, and venture-capital feeder limited partnerships — closed-end, quarterly-marked investment vehicles. These instruments:

- Do **not** trade individual common stocks on a daily basis.
- Do **not** generate 37,238 PTR-reportable transactions across ISRG, PLTR, RTX, BA, NOC, LMT, and similar liquid equity tickers.
- Do **not** provide third-party discretion that would satisfy the SMA-defense premise (an SMA defense requires a broker to be making trade decisions without Member consultation).

The 37,238 common-stock trades reported on the household's PTRs from 2017 through 2026 therefore originate from a custodial account that **is not on the PFD**. Two possibilities exhaust the record:

1. A brokerage account has been **omitted** from the PFD — a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 13104(a)(3), (a)(5), and (a)(8), which together require disclosure of every interest in property, every reportable transaction, and any qualified blind trust through which the household's actively-traded portfolio is held.
2. An SMA's existence has been **concealed** — foreclosing the affirmative defense that premises late-filing on an SMA's notification cadence.

Under either branch, the SMA affirmative defense fails on the face of respondent's own sworn filing.

## 6. Statutory framing

- **5 U.S.C. § 13104(a)(3), (a)(5), and (a)(8)** — disclosure of interests in property, of reportable transactions, and of qualified blind trusts: the conjunction that, applied to the household's 37,238 PTR-reported common-stock trades, requires the underlying custodial account to appear on the PFD asset catalog.
- **5 U.S.C. § 13104(f)(3)** — certification requirements for a qualified blind trust; no qualified-blind-trust certification appears anywhere in the 5-page filing.
- **STOCK Act § 6** / **5 U.S.C. § 13105(l)** — 45-day PTR filing deadline applies irrespective of SMA or broker-notification cadence when no SMA is disclosed on the PFD.
- **Currency note.** The 5 U.S.C. § 13104 and § 13105 cites in this exhibit are the current codification of the Ethics in Government Act under P.L. 117-286 § 7 (Dec. 27, 2022), which renumbered the predecessor 5 U.S.C. App. §§ 102–103 series into 5 U.S.C. Chapter 131. The disclosure obligations on which the exhibit rests — § 13104(a)(3), (a)(5), (a)(8), and (f)(3) — were preserved without substantive amendment by the renumbering. Section 13104(a)(7), which addresses continuing-employment, future-employment, and consulting-agreement disclosures (Schedule F equivalent), is separately current under the same renumbering and is **not** the operative basis for this exhibit's allegations; respondent's Schedule F entries are zero across every reporting year and are addressed elsewhere in the package on the no-forward-arrangement axis.
