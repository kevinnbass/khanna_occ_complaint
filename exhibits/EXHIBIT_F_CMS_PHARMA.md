## 1. Summary

Between May 2017 and August 2024, respondent's household executed pharmaceutical-sector transactions clustering within ±14 calendar days of every catalogued CMS, HHS, and FTC rulemaking event bearing on drug pricing. After per-page re-extraction of every Khanna household PFD Schedule B disclosure, the substrate supports **185 household pharmaceutical trades** distributed across **14 of 14 rulemaking events** in the catalogue — no event yielded a zero-trade window.

The event set spans the Trump-era HHS Drug Pricing Blueprint, the withdrawn International Pricing Index, the Inflation Reduction Act, 340B contract-pharmacy rulemaking, Part D rebate modernization, and the CMS Final Part D Negotiated Drugs List for 2026. The aggregate pattern — 185 pharmaceutical-sector transactions concentrating inside short regulator-action windows across fourteen episodes over seven years, including same-day overlap with four of the nine unique manufacturers whose drug was a negotiation candidate at the IRA Final List window (see § 3) — is documented at the absolute-count and ticker-specificity grain. The complainant does not rest the probative weight of this exhibit on a saturation-rate framing alone (see § 2 candor paragraph) nor on the August 2024 same-day ticker-specificity dimension alone (see § 3 candor paragraph), and instead anchors the showing on (a) the August 2024 same-day overlap occurring inside a thirteen-day pre-publication window for the specific Federal Register publication that named those manufacturers (§ 3), (b) the repeating-pattern characteristic across seven years and fourteen rulemaking episodes (§ 4), (c) the committee-of-jurisdiction nexus (§ 5), and (d) independent third-party tracker corroboration (§ 7).

The **2024-08-15 Final Part D Negotiated Drugs List** episode — within which the household executed twenty-nine pharmaceutical-sector transactions across fifteen distinct manufacturers inside the ±14-day publication window, including a 286-transaction household rebalance executed on a single calendar day (2024-08-02), thirteen days before publication, that itself contained eleven pharmaceutical-sector positions of which four match unique manufacturers whose drug appeared on the published list (AbbVie, Amgen, Johnson & Johnson, Merck) alongside seven additional pharmaceutical-sector positions (Moderna, Regeneron, Vertex, Incyte, Gilead, Biogen, and an aggregated additional pharma-prime line item) — is the headline showing on the same-day ticker-specificity dimension. The household's 2024-08-02 ticker overlap with the IRA list (4 of 9) sits in the same range as the expected hit-rate from a hypergeometric draw of 248 distinct assets against the 9-manufacturer IRA list out of an approximately 503-instrument S&P-500-plus-ADR universe (expected approximately 4.4 hits, observed 4) and is therefore not statistically distinguishable from a same-day rebalance on its own; the probative weight rests on the convergence of (a) the four IRA-list-manufacturer overlap thirteen days pre-publication, (b) the seven-year repeating-pattern characteristic across fourteen rulemaking episodes (§ 4), (c) the Oversight Committee jurisdictional nexus (§ 5), and (d) independent third-party tracker corroboration of eleven of twelve cited 2024-08-02 trades (§ 7). Respondent sat on the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform at all material times; HHS, CMS, and FDA fall within that committee's investigative oversight jurisdiction. The narrower point — that respondent does not sit on the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, the standing committee with primary subject-matter jurisdiction over CMS rulemaking — is acknowledged at § 5, and the Count 3 nexus theory pleaded against respondent does not rest on Energy and Commerce MNPI access.

The CMS IRA Final Part D Negotiated Drugs List for 2026 covers ten drugs from nine unique primary manufacturers (Eliquis — Bristol-Myers Squibb; Jardiance — Eli Lilly co-market; Xarelto — Johnson & Johnson; Januvia — Merck; Farxiga — AstraZeneca; Entresto — Novartis; Enbrel — Amgen; Imbruvica — AbbVie; Stelara — Johnson & Johnson; Fiasp/NovoLog — Novo Nordisk). Of those nine, four (ABBV, AMGN, JNJ, MRK) appear in the 2024-08-02 cluster; five (BMY, LLY, AZN, NVS, NVO) do not. The same-day overlap is therefore four of nine on the unique-manufacturer denominator. The eleven-position 2024-08-02 same-day cluster sits inside the broader twenty-nine-trade / fifteen-manufacturer ±14-day window for this episode catalogued at § 2.

**Candor paragraph — random-rebalance null at the same-day grain.** Under a hypergeometric draw of 248 distinct assets from an approximately 503-instrument S&P-500-plus-ADR universe against a target of 9 IRA-list manufacturers, the expected number of IRA-list hits is approximately n·K/N ≈ 4.44; the observed value is 4. The one-sided probability of observing at least 4 IRA-list hits under this null is approximately 0.60 — that is, the same-day overlap is not statistically distinguishable from the median outcome of a 248-asset random rebalance of this breadth alone. Respondent's household executes broad multi-ticker rebalance days at a cadence of approximately 3.5 per year for rebalances exceeding 200 transactions and roughly one per month for rebalances exceeding 100 transactions, so a four-of-nine ticker overlap on any individual rebalance day against any nine-manufacturer industry sub-list is the expected base-rate outcome at the same-day grain. The probative weight of the August 2024 cluster therefore does not rest on the same-day ticker-specificity dimension standing alone; it rests on the conjunction of (a) the four-of-nine IRA-list manufacturer overlap occurring inside a thirteen-day pre-publication window for the specific Federal Register publication that named those manufacturers, (b) the same convergence pattern repeating across fourteen rulemaking episodes catalogued at § 2 (the seven-year repeating-pattern characteristic at § 4), (c) the Oversight Committee jurisdictional nexus over CMS / HHS / FDA at § 5, and (d) the independent three-way tracker corroboration of eleven of twelve cited 2024-08-02 trades at § 7. The single-cluster null-probability test is reported here for full candor; the multi-axis convergence is reported below.

## 4. Repeating-pattern characteristic

The pattern does not reduce to one anomalous cluster:

- **Fourteen rulemaking episodes**, spanning **seven years** (2017-05-16 through 2024-08-15).
- **Every episode** shows trade concentration inside ±14 days; after per-page re-extraction no event window is empty.
- **Multiple per-event same-day clusters**: 2017-05-02 across twelve manufacturers; 2019-07-01 across three; 2022-01-04 across two; 2023-09-21 across two; 2023-10-02 across nine; 2024-02-13 across eight; **2024-08-02 across eleven pharmaceutical-sector positions, of which four are unique IRA-list manufacturers (see § 3)**.
- **Both BUY and SELL legs appear within event windows**, consistent with event-driven rotation rather than directional long-only conviction.

## 5. Committee-of-jurisdiction nexus

Respondent served on the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform throughout the audit window (committee-assignment record: HSGO seat across the 115th through 119th Congresses). HHS, CMS, FDA, FTC, and ancillary drug-pricing bodies fall within that committee's investigative oversight jurisdiction; respondent's office issued oversight letters and conducted hearings on pharmaceutical manufacturer pricing, pharmacy-benefit-manager conduct, and IRA implementation during 2022 through 2024 — the same window in which the cluster-concentrated trades occurred. Correspondence and hearing records are on file with the Committee.

For full candor: respondent does NOT sit, and has not sat during the audit window, on the House Committee on Energy and Commerce — the standing committee with primary legislative subject-matter jurisdiction over CMS rulemaking, Medicare Part D, and FDA approvals (committee-assignment record cross-referenced against `lake.congress_committee_assignments` for bioguide K000389: HSAS / HSBU / HSGO / HSAG / HSZS only, no HSIF). The Count 3 nexus theory therefore does not rest on Energy-and-Commerce MNPI access. It rests on (a) Oversight investigative jurisdiction over the same agencies, which carries staff-level access to non-public agency briefing material in the ordinary course of an oversight inquiry, and (b) the financial-interest-vs-position-of-trust framing under House Rule XXIII cl. 2 and 5 U.S.C. § 13104(d)(1)(A), which is not gated on E&C committee status.

## 6. Sector profit-and-loss — dual-benchmark disclosure

Pharmaceutical-sector household trading produced positive alpha against the appropriate sector-matched benchmarks and negative alpha against the broad-market SPY benchmark. Both measurements are reported below for full candor; per established Daubert-admissible event-study methodology (MacKinlay 1997 *Journal of Economic Literature* Ch. 4; Campbell, Lo, and MacKinlay 1997 Princeton Ch. 4.2), pharma-sector trade-selection alpha is properly measured against a sector-matched benchmark (XLV health-care, IBB biotech, or VHT total health-care) rather than against a broad-market index, because the pharmaceutical sector exhibited material drift relative to SPY across the 2017–2026 audit window.

Strict pharmaceutical universe (12 tickers — ABBV, AMGN, BIIB, GILD, INCY, JNJ, LLY, MRK, MRNA, PFE, REGN, VRTX; 1,088 priced transactions; midpoint notional $11.72M; terminal mark anchor 2026-04-17):

| Benchmark | Baseline P&L | Alpha vs household actual P&L (+$3,175,976) |
|---|---:|---:|
| SPY (broad market) | +$3,422,735 | **−$246,759** |
| XLV (health-care sector ETF) | +$1,319,675 | **+$1,856,301** |
| IBB (biotech sector ETF) | +$1,380,730 | **+$1,795,246** |
| VHT (total health-care sector ETF) | +$1,295,498 | **+$1,880,478** |
| Matched composite (XLV for big pharma + IBB for biotech) | — | **+$1,837,077** |

Broader pharmaceutical-plus-devices-plus-services universe (20 tickers; 1,835 priced transactions; midpoint notional $19.56M):

| Benchmark | Baseline P&L | Alpha vs household actual P&L (+$4,262,913) |
|---|---:|---:|
| SPY (broad market) | +$5,698,292 | **−$1,435,378** |
| Matched sector composite (XLV / IBB / VHT) | — | **+$2,138,624** |

The directional reading is consistent across both universes: under any sector-matched benchmark — XLV, IBB, VHT, or the matched composite — household pharma-portfolio alpha is **positive in the +$1.8M to +$2.1M range** depending on universe scope, while under the SPY broad-market benchmark alpha is negative in the −$0.25M to −$1.4M range. The roughly $1.8M to $2.1M swing between SPY-alpha and sector-matched-alpha measures pharmaceutical-sector drift against the broad market over the audit window — an ambient sector-return phenomenon — and is not attributable to household trade-selection skill or deficit. The sector-matched measurement is the appropriate Daubert-admissible measurement for a trade-selection-alpha question on a pharma-sector universe.

The probative weight of this Count does not turn on whether household pharma alpha is positive or negative. The regulatory-timing signal asserted at §3 (August 2024 same-day ticker-specificity), §4 (seven-year repeating pattern), §5 (committee-of-jurisdiction nexus), and §7 (third-party tracker corroboration) is **independent of directional accuracy** — the household derived an information advantage by **trading through** the events, not by **betting on their direction**. Sector-matched positive alpha is consistent with the regulatory-timing hypothesis; SPY-relative negative alpha is also consistent with it. The complainant flags the dual-benchmark result here so the Committee and any reviewing court have the honest picture rather than a benchmark-cherry-picked single number.

## 7. Independent corroboration against public trade trackers

The twelve CMS August 2024 trades listed in §3 were cross-validated against the two largest independent Congressional-trade trackers (Capitol Trades; QuiverQuant). Per-trade match on transaction date (±1 day), ticker, owner, and transaction type:

- **Eleven of twelve CMS August 2024 trades are corroborated 3-of-3** (lake disclosure + Capitol Trades + QuiverQuant).
- **The twelfth trade** — originally indexed as an SP PFE transaction on 2024-08-02 — was re-anchored during a tx-grain canonical-view rebuild to the same household's SP PFE transaction on **2024-08-23** (eight days after publication rather than thirteen days before). The canonical tx is present in the House Clerk filing; the earlier date was a pre-canonical-view index artifact. The underlying PFE transaction is a real filing; only its window-offset changed.

Independent validation across two third-party trackers directly contradicts any defense that the cited trades are extraction artifacts or fabrications. Cross-validation is documented per-trade in `EXHIBIT_FF_3WAY_AGREEMENT.md`.
