# Exhibit DD — The California-state mirror: Q1-2026 crypto-industry state-level mobilization, parallel to the federal PROTECT PROGRESS pipeline

**Primary sources.** California Secretary of State Cal-Access bulk export (https://campaignfinance.cdn.sos.ca.gov/dbwebexport.zip); Cal-Access tables for recipient-committee organization (`lake.ca_cal_access_cvr_so_cd`), filer name (`lake.ca_cal_access_filername_cd`), contribution receipts (`lake.ca_cal_access_rcpt_cd`), filer-to-filings linkage (`lake.ca_cal_access_filer_filings_cd`), and Form 497 late-contribution disclosures (`lake.ca_cal_access_s497_cd`); San Francisco Ethics Commission Campaign Finance Transactions disclosure (DataSF dataset PITQ-E56W, `lake.ca_sfgov_org_campaign_finance_transactions_pitq_e56w`).

## 1. Summary

Across the first quarter of 2026, the same cryptocurrency-industry principals who finance the federal Super-PAC pipeline documented in Exhibits G, P, W, and BB concurrently deployed **at least $18.75 million across four California state-level political committees**, operating through **two structurally independent compliance stacks**. The state-layer deployment mirrors the federal pattern — same donor households, same temporal window, same policy posture-seeking behavior — but routes through California's own disclosure regime rather than the FEC's. A separate San Francisco city-grain disclosure vertical captures a third stream of Mr. Larsen's political spending: $10.9 million in disclosed contributions at the SF-city level across 2019–2026, bringing his combined disclosed state-and-city-grain mobilization to approximately $25.65 million.

The state-and-city mirror supplies structural-continuity context for the federal coordination question that Count 4 of the accompanying Office of Congressional Conduct complaint and the companion Federal Election Commission referral place at issue: the donor cohort financing the federal PROTECT PROGRESS slate (Exhibit P) is the same donor cohort that activated a parallel state-level network on an essentially identical timeline.

## 3. Two parallel compliance stacks

A distinctive feature of the state-layer network is that the four committees do **not** share a single treasurer or administrative office. Instead, they route through two structurally independent compliance infrastructures operating in parallel.

**Stack A — Nielsen Merksamer / Bank of Marin / San Rafael 94901.**

- Building A Better California (filer 1486767) — Treasurer Elli Abdoli; filing contact `FORM410@NMGOVLAW.COM` (Nielsen Merksamer Parrinello Gross & Leoni, a major California political-compliance firm); bank Bank of Marin; registered address San Rafael, California 94901.
- California Business Roundtable Issues PAC (filer 1264590) — Treasurer Steven S. Lucas; same Nielsen Merksamer / Bank of Marin / San Rafael 94901 stack. This PAC is a long-running ballot-measure committee whose donor roster historically reflects traditional California business interests; its Q1-2026 $750,000 infusion from Mr. Larsen is a material change in the character of its funder base.

**Stack B — Bison Strategies / Amalgamated Bank / Sacramento 95814.**

- Golden State Promise (filer 1486695) — Treasurer Brandon Philipczyk, the same individual who serves as treasurer of PROTECT PROGRESS and FAIRSHAKE at the federal level (see Exhibit BB §§2–3); filing contact Bison Strategies; bank Amalgamated Bank (Washington, D.C.); registered address Sacramento, California 95814. This is the first California-state committee for which Mr. Philipczyk has been disclosed as treasurer, and it extends his multi-committee compliance practice from the federal layer into the California-state layer.

The two-stack pattern is structurally unusual. A single donor household (Mr. Larsen / Ripple Labs) distributes $14.75 million across three committees in the same two-week window, deliberately routing two of those committees through one compliance firm and the third through an entirely different compliance firm, bank, and treasurer. The practical effect is that the state-layer network is not a single committee that the California Fair Political Practices Commission or the California Attorney General can evaluate as one operation, but rather two parallel operations whose common origin lies only at the donor tier.

### 4.1 The state-level participating cohort: Larsen, Armstrong, and Haun Ventures

A 16-cluster cryptocurrency-industry probe of California Form 497 late-contribution records identifies the state-level participating cohort. The probe scope was defined under three conjunctive selection criteria, applied at the cluster-identification stage prior to any Cal-Access query: (i) every employer that contributed $250,000 or more to PROTECT PROGRESS or FAIRSHAKE in 2024 (per Exhibit G §2 and Exhibit BB §3); (ii) the largest publicly identified cryptocurrency exchanges and trading platforms by 2024 disclosed assets under management; and (iii) the largest publicly identified crypto-focused venture-capital and infrastructure firms by deployed capital. The sixteen clusters that satisfied at least one of the three criteria are: Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), Blockchain, Chainalysis, Circle, Coinbase, Filecoin, Gemini, Haun Ventures, Jump Crypto, Kraken, Pantera Capital, Paradigm, Polygon, Ripple, Solana Labs, and Uniswap.

The probe filter against `lake.ca_cal_access_s497_cd` Form 497 late-contribution records was `entity_cd = 'IND'` (individual donors) with `amount >= 1000`, evaluated across the 2024–2026 window. The state-level participating cohort comprises three clusters: **Ripple** (approximately $21.2 million raw / $15.8 million deduplicated, dominated by Mr. Larsen), **Coinbase** ($40,000 across four events), and **Haun Ventures** ($8,000 across two events). The state-level participating cohort is therefore narrower than the federal PROTECT PROGRESS funder list, with Mr. Larsen and Mr. Armstrong driving the state layer and the remainder of the crypto-industry donor universe routing through federal rather than state channels.

## 6. The California mirror as structural-continuity context for the federal coordination question

The California state-and-city mirror supplies three forms of structural-continuity record evidence for the federal coordination question set out in Count 4 of the accompanying Office of Congressional Conduct complaint and in the companion Federal Election Commission referral:

1. **Continuity of donor identity.** The Q1-2026 state-layer $18.75 million and the 2024-cycle federal $6.78 million PROTECT PROGRESS receipts (Exhibit G §2) are drawn from an overlapping donor cohort. Mr. Larsen / Ripple Labs and Mr. Armstrong / Coinbase are the two largest participants in both layers.
2. **Continuity of administrative infrastructure.** The Bison Strategies / Amalgamated Bank / Sacramento stack servicing Golden State Promise is operated by the same individual, Mr. Philipczyk, who serves as treasurer for FAIRSHAKE and PROTECT PROGRESS at the federal level (Exhibit BB §2). The compliance infrastructure is continuous across the jurisdictional boundary.
3. **Continuity of policy-posture-seeking behavior.** The California state-level committees include a gubernatorial independent-expenditure committee (Mahan for Governor 2026) supporting a candidate who, if elected, would seat a state-level executive whose appointees would administer California's crypto licensing regime in parallel with the federal regime that Representative Khanna's vote on the Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act (FIT21) addressed. The same donor cohort is thus disclosed as a participant at both the state-level and federal-level regulatory layers within the same temporal window.
