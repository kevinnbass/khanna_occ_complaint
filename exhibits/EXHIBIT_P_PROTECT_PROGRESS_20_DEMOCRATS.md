## 3. Vote alignment on the industry's signature legislation

### FIT21 (H.R. 4763) — House roll call 226, May 22, 2024

The Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act ("FIT21") was a cryptocurrency-industry legislative priority in the 118th Congress. It moves primary jurisdiction over digital-commodity trading from the Securities and Exchange Commission to the Commodity Futures Trading Commission and removes SEC oversight from many tokens. Coinbase, Ripple, and Andreessen Horowitz — the three largest PROTECT PROGRESS funders and four of its eight named donors — each publicly lobbied for passage.

Of the twenty slate beneficiaries, ten were voting House Members at the time of the roll call (the other ten were non-incumbent challengers). Those ten: Khanna, Gomez, Thanedar, Torres, Menendez Jr., Goldman, Gottheimer, Kennedy (a freshly-sworn House Member following the April 30, 2024 NY-26 special election), Slotkin (then a House Member running for the open Michigan Senate seat), and Gallego (then a House Member running for the open Arizona Senate seat). All ten voted YEA on FIT21 — every PROTECT PROGRESS beneficiary who cast a FIT21 vote cast it in favor of the industry's bill.

#### Within-cohort concentration of support

Read in the inverse direction, the same record establishes that PROTECT PROGRESS's support was selective rather than uniform across the FIT21-aligned cohort. **Seventy-one House Democrats voted Aye on FIT21 passage**, the bill's supporting cohort within the majority caucus. Of those seventy-one, **ten** received any PROTECT PROGRESS independent expenditure during the 2024 cycle: Representative Khanna, Representative Gomez, Representative Thanedar, Representative Torres, Representative Menendez Jr., Representative Goldman, Representative Gottheimer, Representative Kennedy, Representative Slotkin, and Representative Gallego. **Sixty-one of the seventy-one received zero PROTECT PROGRESS support.**

That 10-of-71 within-cohort concentration — approximately fourteen and one-tenth percent — is the structural fact that distinguishes the pattern from a generalized issue-aligned-giving model. If PROTECT PROGRESS were funding all FIT21-supportive Members at scale, the support would distribute proportionately across the seventy-one Aye votes — instead, it concentrates on roughly one in seven of them. The selectivity is not explained by the FIT21 vote alone; that vote is the entry condition (the ten are a subset of the seventy-one, not a counter-example to ideological alignment), but it is not a sufficient condition. Some additional selection criterion is doing work between "voted Aye on FIT21" (true of seventy-one Members) and "received PROTECT PROGRESS independent-expenditure support" (true of ten). Whether that additional criterion is committee assignment, fundraising-cycle timing, donor-personal-relationship preference, or a coordination element subject to the 11 C.F.R. § 109.21 standard is precisely the question the Federal Election Commission's investigative authority under 52 U.S.C. § 30107(a)(3) can develop.

### SAB 121 (H.J.Res. 109) — House roll calls 189 (May 8, 2024) and 355 (July 11, 2024)

H.J.Res. 109 was a Congressional Review Act resolution disapproving SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin 121, which required banks to balance-sheet customer crypto-custody assets — a rule the cryptocurrency industry sought to eliminate. The House initially passed the resolution on May 8, 2024 (Roll 189) and overrode President Biden's veto sixty-four days later on July 11, 2024 (Roll 355). Both votes were on identical resolution text; no intervening textual amendment occurred between the two.

The vote-pattern split among the ten voting slate Members across both occasions partitions cleanly into three structural classes — **YEA-on-both (4 Members), NAY-on-both (4 Members), NAY-then-YEA flip (2 Members)**:

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **YEA-YEA (4)** | Slotkin (MI; House → MI Senate run) | YEA | YEA | YEA on both |
| | Gallego (AZ; House → AZ Senate run) | YEA | YEA | YEA on both |
| | Torres (NY-15) | YEA | YEA | YEA on both |
| | Gottheimer (NJ-5) | YEA | YEA | YEA on both |
| **NAY-NAY (4)** | Gomez (CA-34) | NAY | NAY | NAY on both |
| | Goldman (NY-10) | NAY | NAY | NAY on both |
| | Menendez Jr. (NJ-8) | NAY | NAY | NAY on both |
| | Kennedy (NY-26) | NAY | NAY | NAY on both |
| **NAY → YEA (2)** | **Khanna (CA-17)** | **NAY** | **YEA** | **flipped to industry** |
| | **Thanedar (MI-13)** | **NAY** | **YEA** | **flipped to industry** |

Four of the ten voting beneficiaries voted with the industry on both occasions; four voted against the industry on both occasions; two — Representative Khanna and Representative Thanedar — voted against the industry on initial passage and then with the industry on the override sixty-four days later. The 4 / 4 / 2 partition is the structural framework into which the candidate-side vote-of-record evidence on the 2024 SAB 121 axis sorts.

The 4 / 4 / 2 partition is itself probative that the slate-cohort vote position on SAB 121 is not uniform. The PAC's 2024-cycle independent-expenditure deployment in support of these ten voting Members did not require pre-supporting Members to hold a uniform pro-industry position on the SAB 121 axis specifically — four of the ten voted with the industry on both occasions, four voted against the industry on both occasions, and two flipped between the two occasions. The non-uniformity within the slate cuts against any framing that the slate's recipients held a single coordinated industry position on this regulatory vehicle. The probative weight of the NAY-YEA flip pair (Khanna and Thanedar) is heightened by the fact that the same PAC that supported them with independent expenditures also supported four Members who voted against the industry on both votes and four who voted with the industry on both votes; the flip is therefore an individual-Member position-shift, not a slate-wide pattern.

The personal-retail-channel donor record across the twenty-Member slate set out at § 2 of this Exhibit, when rolled forward from the 2024-cycle baseline to the 2026-cycle to-date inflow record, supplies an additional feature relevant to this Referral. Aggregating principal-campaign-committee personal-retail Schedule A inflow at the per-Member grain, the slate-wide personal-retail channel collapsed approximately seventy-nine percent between the 2024-cycle and the 2026-cycle to-date cut, reflecting the cycle-completion partial-data state for sitting-Member committees in mid-2026. The Member-by-Member drop pattern is highly heterogeneous. Representative Khanna's principal campaign committee records the **smallest percentage drop** of any of the twenty slate Members at approximately negative fifteen percent — substantially below the slate-wide median percentage drop and substantially below every other slate Member's percentage drop on the same axis. The smallest-drop ranking on the 2026-cycle personal-retail channel cuts against any framing that the post-2024-cycle donor relationship to Representative Khanna abandoned the candidate after the SAB 121 / FIT21 vote-cycle closed; the personal-retail channel is, on the available 2026-cycle data, more resilient at Representative Khanna's principal campaign committee than at any other 2024-cycle slate beneficiary's committee. The structural-significance question this Referral asks the Commission to weigh is whether the post-vote-window personal-retail-channel resilience documented above, taken together with the upstream FAIRSHAKE affiliate-transfer pipeline at § 2.1.1 of the FEC Referral and the post-flip senior-executive contribution surge documented below, supports the conduct-prong inference under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(d) on the 2024-cycle facts of record.

#### The Khanna intra-2024 vote-flip on identical resolution text

Representative Khanna's position on H.J.Res. 109 reversed within a single sixty-four-day window in 2024 on a resolution whose text never changed. On May 8, 2024 he voted **NAY** on initial passage of the SAB 121 disapproval resolution. On July 11, 2024 he voted **YEA** on the override of President Biden's veto of the very same resolution. The override vote failed (the two-thirds threshold was not reached), but the position-flip on identical legislative language is the cleanest candidate-side conduct evidence available on the SAB 121 axis: same resolution, opposite vote, no doctrinal textual change to explain the shift. Companion procedural-Rule votes the day before initial passage — Roll 180 (Previous Question, May 7, 2024) and Roll 181 (Resolution Agreement, May 7, 2024) — also drew Nay votes from Representative Khanna, consistent with his initial-passage Nay rather than with the override-vote Yea that followed sixty-four days later.

#### Donor-flow envelope around the sixty-four-day flip window

Filing-period contribution receipts to Representative Khanna's principal campaign committee (Ro For Congress, C00503185) from cryptocurrency-industry employers and crypto-occupation contributors are documented in three windows around the H.J.Res. 109 flip.

**Pre-flip baseline (2023-01-01 through 2024-05-07; sixteen-month window).** Twenty-eight itemized contributions totaling approximately $78,800 from a sustained crypto-industry executive donor pattern, including Ripple Executive Chairman Chris Larsen ($11,600 across four contributions), Ripple CEO Brad Garlinghouse ($6,600 across two), Andreessen Horowitz General Partner Chris Dixon ($6,600 across two), Andreessen Horowitz Co-Founder Ben Horowitz ($6,600 across two), Blockchain Capital Managing Partner Bart P. Stephens ($6,600 across two), Blockchain Association CEO Kristin Smith ($6,600 across two), Coinbase Attorney Paul Grewal ($6,600 across two), Wave Digital Assets investor Les Borsai ($6,600 across two), Paradigm Global President Amed Khan ($3,300), and Crypto Council executive Sheila Warren ($2,000). The pattern is one of senior-executive-grade direct giving sustained at meaningful amounts across the sixteen months preceding the May 8 Nay.

**Flip-window (2024-05-08 through 2024-07-11; the sixty-four days between Roll 189 and Roll 355).** Two contributions total. Khaja Ahmed (Gemini executive) gave $6,600 on June 22, 2024 — the contribution carries a "REATTRIBUTION OR REFUND TO TAKE PLACE WITHIN 60 DAYS" memo and was followed on August 22, 2024 by a $3,300 reattribution adjustment, consistent with a routine joint-account-attribution disposition rather than with new-money flow. Alden Knowlton (Coinbase Senior Policy Manager) gave $500 on June 26, 2024, fifteen days before the override vote. The flip-window is structurally quiet: the donor relationship that had been sustained at C-suite-grade dollar magnitudes through May 7, 2024 paused during the period the candidate's position on H.J.Res. 109 was reversing.

**Post-flip surge (2024-07-12 through 2024-11-20; the four months following the override vote).** Twelve distinct senior-executive contributions totaling approximately $36,000+, materializing within twenty-five days of the override Yea and continuing through the November 2024 election cycle: Coinbase President Emilie Choi ($3,300 on August 5, twenty-five days post-flip); Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong ($3,300 on August 7, twenty-seven days post-flip); Blockchain Association CEO Kristin Smith ($3,300 on September 21); Ripple Attorney Stuart Alderoty ($3,300 on September 24); Coinbase Lawyer Molly Abraham ($1,000 on September 25); Circle executive Dante Disparte ($3,300 on September 26); Andreessen Horowitz investor Anjney Midha ($3,300 on September 29); Blockchain Capital General Counsel Joshua Rivera ($1,000 on September 30); Andreessen Horowitz Partner Martin Casado ($3,300 on November 5); Paradigm Co-Founder Fred Ehrsam ($3,300 on November 5); Kraken CEO Arjun Sethi ($3,300 on November 5); and Andreessen Horowitz investor Vijay Pande ($3,300 on November 20).

The pre-flip-versus-post-flip donor identity is structurally non-overlapping at the senior-executive grain. The CEOs and Presidents who appear in the post-flip surge — Armstrong (Coinbase), Choi (Coinbase), Sethi (Kraken), Ehrsam (Paradigm), Casado (Andreessen Horowitz) — had not previously contributed to Ro For Congress before the override Yea. The senior executives who had maxed out before May 8, 2024 — Garlinghouse (Ripple) and Larsen (Ripple) — were exhausted to the FECA individual maximum and accordingly did not appear in the post-flip surge regardless of whether they would have given more had a higher cap allowed it. The empirical sequence — sustained pre-flip relationship at C-suite grade; quiet sixty-four-day flip-window; new C-suite executives initiating contributions within twenty-five days of the override Yea — is consistent with a candidate-side pivot in policy posture toward the industry's regulatory advocacy.

#### Independent corroboration: PROTECT PROGRESS Schedule E timing

PROTECT PROGRESS's two 2024 independent expenditures supporting Representative Khanna — the $100,000.00 media buy paid to Targeted Platform Media LLC on October 4, 2024 and the $8,545.79 media production payment to Backstory Strategies LLC on October 21, 2024, set out in §2.1 above — are dated approximately three months after the Roll 355 override Yea on July 11, 2024. The PROTECT PROGRESS independent-expenditure timing is downstream of the vote-flip rather than contemporaneous bridge-window cause; in conduct-prong terms under 11 C.F.R. § 109.21(d), the IE timing is consistent with post-flip reward sequencing rather than pre-flip inducement. The Schedule E timing thereby attaches the §109.21(d)(4) and (d)(5) candidate-side conduct evidence to the same resolution on which the position-flip occurred and on which the donor-flow surge concentrated.

The combined record on H.J.Res. 109 — identical-text intra-2024 vote-flip; sustained pre-flip senior-executive donor relationship that quieted across the sixty-four-day flip-window; new-CEO contribution surge initiated within twenty-five days of the override Yea; PROTECT PROGRESS independent expenditures landing approximately three months after the override Yea — places SAB 121 alongside FIT21 as a roll-call dyad on which the donor-vote relationship is documentary and contemporaneous, not inferred.

## 5. The pattern

- PROTECT PROGRESS is funded entirely by cryptocurrency-industry principals.
- It spent $34.10 million supporting 20 Democratic candidates in 2024.
- Each of those beneficiaries who was in a position to vote on FIT21 — the industry's signature bill — voted for it.
- Of the slate's 20 candidates, Representative Khanna's principal committee shows the greatest overlap with the PROTECT PROGRESS donor universe; 40 of the Super PAC's named donors also gave directly to Ro For Congress.
- Representative Khanna is the only slate beneficiary who sits on the House Armed Services Committee and holds disclosed household assets spanning the sectors that FIT21 and SAB 121 regulate.

Under *Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310 (2010), independent expenditures are constitutionally protected only in the absence of coordination. The coordination test at 11 C.F.R. § 109.21 reaches material involvement, common vendors, donor-conduit arrangements, and temporal donor-overlap indicia. The pattern documented above — a single-industry Super PAC whose donors simultaneously exhaust the individual contribution limit to the beneficiary's candidate committee, followed within five months by the beneficiary's favorable vote on the industry's signature legislation — presents the donor-universe overlap indicia that *McConnell v. FEC*, 540 U.S. 93 (2003), identified as probative of coordination. Whether PROTECT PROGRESS's independent expenditures were in fact independent of Representative Khanna's campaign is a question that the Federal Election Commission, with its subpoena authority over communications, vendors, and strategic planning, is positioned to resolve.
