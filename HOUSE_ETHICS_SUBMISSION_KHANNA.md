## 2. Legal framework

### 2.1 Ethics in Government Act scope

3. Under 5 U.S.C. § 13104(d)(1)(A), a Member's Annual PFD must disclose the spouse's earned and unearned income, including gifts and donations of property and unearned income derived from spouse-held assets or interests. The "spouse-held asset" scope has been interpreted by the Office of Government Ethics in Letter Opinion 03-2, and by the Department of Justice in Advisory Opinion 170, to include foundation assets over which the spouse exercises dispositive authority as a trustee. The Committee has historically addressed analogous spouse-trustee questions on a case-by-case basis under House Rule XXIII.

4. Under 5 U.S.C. § 13104(f)(3), the disclosure exemption for a qualified blind trust, exempt investment fund, or qualified trust is conditioned on certification of the trust and on the Member's lack of knowledge of specific trust investments. A Member's public representation that household trading is managed by a separately-managed-account structure triggers the disclosure expectation that an actual SMA, if one exists, must be documented on the PFD record.

### 2.2 Statutory penalty framework

5. Under 5 U.S.C. § 13106, knowing-and-willful failure to file or filing falsely is subject to civil penalties; the Committee retains discretion to recommend such penalties in its findings. Any finding that knowing-and-willful concealment rises to a materially false statement in a federal filing implicates 18 U.S.C. § 1001 as the parallel criminal framework; § 1001 is referenced here only as the statutory counterpart available on any Committee referral path, not as a charging theory advanced in this submission.

### 3.2 The separately-managed-account representation against the margin-loan and written-options record

12. Respondent has publicly attributed household trading to a separately-managed-account structure. Direct Tesseract optical character recognition of respondent's most recent Annual PFD (House Clerk document 8221318, filed February 4, 2026 across five pages) discloses **no** SMA broker, **no** qualified blind trust, and **no** third-party discretionary custodian across the Schedule A account-custodian schedule [OCC_M005, OCC_M065]. The filing lists direct brokerage ownership of private-equity and hedge-fund access limited partnerships in family-trust wrappers without any "managed by" or "discretion" annotation. The same closed family-trust structure is independently confirmed by a page-level audit of the household's full Periodic Transaction Report record across all 114 PTRs filed during respondent's House tenure: every reportable transaction in that corpus attributes to one of approximately a dozen named Ahuja- or Ahuja-Khanna-family trusts (including the Ritu Ahuja 1994 Trust, the Ritu Ahuja 1995 Trust, the Ahuja Khanna Children Irrevocable Trust, the Monte and Usha Ahuja 2010 Irrevocable Trust FBO Grandchildren, the Ritu Ahuja Declaration of Trust, the Ahuja Grandchildren's Education Trust, the 2020 Trust FBO Khanna Children, and related vehicles), and **none** to any third-party Schwab, Fidelity, Vanguard, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, or Merrill brokerage account or to any SEC-registered investment-adviser-managed SMA. The PFD-and-PTR record therefore agree that no third-party discretionary structure exists. *See* Exhibit L.

13. A structured re-extraction of respondent's Annual PFD Schedule D filings for tax years 2017 through 2020 reconciles the household's margin-loan record as follows [OCC_M028, OCC_M058]:

   - TY2017 (House Clerk document 9111561): Goldman Sachs margin, "over $1,000,000" band, single liability; emergent ten months after respondent's January 3, 2017 swearing-in.
   - TY2018 (document 9113638): two Goldman Sachs margin entries combining $265,000–$550,000.
   - TY2019 (document 9115242): a $100,001–$250,000 margin entry from the Ritu Ahuja 1994 Trust (a spouse-affiliated family trust), alongside a Goldman Sachs margin entry of $250,001–$500,000.
   - TY2020 (document 8217503): combined margin exposure of $500,001–$1,000,000 across two liabilities.

   All four years code the margin creditors as spouse-owned ("SP"). The same spouse-owned accounts record active written-options activity across the Periodic Transaction Report record for the same period — the sale (not the purchase) of option contracts, a customer-initiated transaction class under Regulation T and FINRA Rule 4210 that is structurally incompatible with a passive SMA or QBT posture. The companion principal complaint documents 282 distinct XSP (S&P 500 mini-index) PUT-option transactions across respondent's tenure at the same spouse-owned account, each band-disclosed $1,001–$15,000, with strikes systematically out of the money and expiries rolling continuously — the footprint of a cash-secured or margined short-volatility premium-harvest program.

The Committee may wish to examine each Annual PFD from TY2017 through TY2024 to determine whether any year's filing asserted an SMA or QBT inconsistent with the 2024 filing, and whether respondent's public statements regarding SMA or QBT structure are reconcilable with the margin-and-written-options disclosure record. ### 3.4 The Dover, Delaware rental — ten-year asymmetric disclosure

18. **The asymmetry — three structural legs.** The re-extraction of respondent's Annual PFDs for TY2014 through TY2023 returns the following on the Dover rental [OCC_M057, OCC_M073]:

   (A) **Schedule A asset-side ten-year omission.** No Schedule A real-estate asset entry referencing "Dover," "Delaware," or "First Bank of Wilmington" appears on any of the ten Annual PFDs.

   (B) **Schedule D liability disclosure unpaired with any asset.** Respondent's TY2021 Annual PFD Schedule D (House Clerk document 8219143) discloses a $100,001–$250,000 mortgage liability on the Dover, Delaware rental to First Bank of Wilmington, Delaware. The mortgage is disclosed without a companion Schedule A asset entry in the same or any adjacent year, making the asset-liability pairing structurally absent and independently probative of a § 13104(a)(3) defect.

   (C) **Six paired-disclosure exemplars in the same ten-PFD window.** Six other rental properties held by respondent — the Cincinnati, Ohio rental; the Denham, Louisiana rental; the Walton Hills, Ohio rental; the Harahan, Louisiana commercial rental; the Ritu Ahuja Revocable Trust New York condominium; and the Walton, Ohio rental — are correctly paired with Schedule A asset entries and with Schedule A, B, or C income entries in the same ten-PFD window. The filer household plainly understands the correct disclosure pattern.

19. **Rebuttal to "reasonable interpretation" defense.** The six-of-seven paired-correctly exemplars rebut any claim that respondent or his preparer held a good-faith "reasonable interpretation" that rental-property assets need not be disclosed when a corresponding mortgage appears on Schedule D.

## 4. Specific questions presented for the Committee's consideration

20. **Question 1 — Spouse-held Foundation asset within § 13104(d)(1)(A).** Whether the Ahuja Charitable Foundation's approximately $45 million corpus [OCC_M055], or any subset thereof, is a "spouse-held asset" or "spouse-controlled interest" within 5 U.S.C. § 13104(d)(1)(A), given Ritu Ahuja Khanna's trustee role on Form 990-PF Part VIII for tax years 2018 through 2024 — seven consecutive tax years constituting the full window of the Foundation's e-file 990-PF filings in the IRS Tax Exempt Organization e-file corpus [OCC_M054] — and whether respondent's Annual PFDs for 2017 through 2024 reflect that holding consistent with the Committee's interpretation of the statute. Respondent is not himself named in Part VIII of any Foundation Form 990-PF across that seven-tax-year window; the Committee is asked to consider the scope question on a spouse-trustee basis only, without reliance on any premise that respondent himself holds a trustee role.

21. **Question 2 — SMA and qualified-blind-trust consistency under § 13104(f)(3).** Whether respondent's public statements regarding SMA or QBT management of household accounts are reconcilable with the documented Annual PFD filings, given (i) the Goldman Sachs margin-loan facility disclosed across TY2017 through TY2020 Schedule D and reconciled at § 3.2 above, (ii) the active written-PUT-option transactions in the same spouse-owned accounts, and (iii) the absence of any qualified-blind-trust certification on any Annual Schedule A. If no SMA or QBT exists on the record, whether any prior Annual PFD asserted the existence of one in a manner warranting correction.

22. **Question 3 — Spouse substantial-contributor reporting under § 13104(a).** Whether § 13104(a) spouse-income disclosure includes reporting of the spouse's substantial-contributor status to the Foundation when the spouse simultaneously holds a trustee interest. If the Committee determines such reporting is required, identification of the specific filings in which disclosure is absent.

23. **Question 4 — Non-cash property transfer reporting.** Whether the tax-year-2024 10,076-share NVIDIA transfer (reported at $1,667,345 donation-time fair market value per IRS 990-PF Schedule B) from respondent's spouse and from the Monte and Usha Ahuja Family Trust to the Foundation [OCC_M027] triggered any § 13104 disclosure obligation on respondent's PFD as an item of spouse unearned income or as a gift-relation disclosure.

24. **Question 5 — Dover, Delaware rental property asset-and-income non-disclosure under §§ 13104(a)(1)(B) and 13104(a)(3).** Whether respondent's Annual PFDs for TY2014 through TY2023 satisfy §§ 13104(a)(1)(B) (interest in property) and § 13104(a)(3) (unearned income) with respect to the Dover rental for which a $100,001–$250,000 mortgage liability to First Bank of Wilmington, Delaware is disclosed on TY2021 Schedule D while no Schedule A asset entry and no Schedule B or C rental-income entry appears in any of the ten Annual PFDs [OCC_M057, OCC_M073]. If the Committee determines the structural asymmetry reflects reporting defects, identification of the specific filings requiring correction and the scope of any § 13106 penalty analysis.
